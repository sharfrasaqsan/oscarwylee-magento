<h2 class="subtitle" id="definition">What are Photochromic Lenses?</h2>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses are <b>a type of glasses lens that are clear when inside or in the shade but will darken to resemble the tint of sunglasses when they are exposed to sunlight</b>. A photochromic lens is coated with a chemical that darkens in response to direct ultraviolet light that is emitted from the sun. Photochromic lenses are suitable for people who need the vision correction provided by normal prescription glasses but do not want to switch between prescription glasses and sunglasses when moving inside and outside. Photochromic lenses adapt to lighting conditions meaning a person can have sunglasses and prescription glasses all in one. In summary, the answer to what are photochromic lenses is they are a type of glasses lens that will change from clear to dark when exposed to direct sunlight.</p>

<p class="para-content">Yes, photochromic lenses <b>can also be known as transition lenses</b>. Transition lenses generally refer to the brand Transitions, which makes photochromic lenses.</p>

<h3 class="con-title">What are Photochromic Lenses Made Of?</h3>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses will be <b>made of the same material normal glasses are made of, such as glass or plastic, but will have an additional chemical embedded or coated onto the lens</b>. The chemical used in some of the first photochromic lenses was silver chloride, however, proprietary or photochromic dyes are now more commonly used.</p>
</div>
<div class="sub-div">
<h3 class="con-title" id="how-they-work">How do Photochromic Lenses Work?</h3>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses <b>work through a chemical reaction</b>. The dyes in a photochromic lens will undergo a chemical reaction when exposed to UV light, which darkens the lens, and then reverses when the UV light is taken away, according to the American Academy of Ophthalmology.</p>

<h3 class="con-title">Why do Photochromic Lenses Change Colour Under Sunlight?</h3>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses <b>change colour under sunlight, as sunlight contains ultraviolet (UV) light</b>. Ultraviolet or UV light is a type of light emitted from the sun. UV light has a shorter wavelength than visible light and cannot be seen by humans. UV light is classified into 3 types; ultraviolet A (UVA), ultraviolet B (UVB), and ultraviolet C (UVC), according to the Centres for Disease Control and Prevention. Photochromic glasses turn dark in the sun as UV light causes a reaction in the chemicals within the lens, turning them dark.</p>

<h3 class="con-title" id="uses">What is the Primary Use of Photochromic Lenses?</h3>
<p class="para-content">The primary use of photochromic lenses <b>is to provide both the vision correction of optical glasses and the UV protection of sunglasses</b>. Photochromic lenses are typically prescription glasses that will turn from clear to dark when exposed to ultraviolet light from the sun. The benefit of photochromic lenses is that people do not need to switch between normal glasses and sunglasses, instead they get the benefits of both in one.</p>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Can Photochromic Lenses be Used as Sunglasses?</h4>
<p class="para-content">Yes, photochromic lenses <b>can generally be used as sunglasses for the purpose of UV protection</b>. Photochromic lenses are designed to change from clear to dark when exposed to ultraviolet light. Photochromic lenses are essentially glasses that turn into sunglasses when in the sun. The dark photochromic lens will work in the same way as the tint on regular sunglasses, helping to protect the eyes from UV radiation emitted from the sun. However, if a person requires additional protection from their sunglasses, such as protection from glare in the form of polarisation, this may not be included. It is important to check with the retailer of your photochromic lenses to be certain of what protection they offer so you can ensure your eye care needs are being met.</p>
</div>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Can Photochromic Lenses be Polarised?</h4>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses <b>are not generally polarised</b>. Photochromic lenses turn from clear to dark tint when exposed to UV light from the sun. Polarised lenses are designed to help protect the eyes from glare via a filter or coating added to the lens that blocks horizontal light. Polarisation is not often added to photochromic lenses as polarisation is not needed for normal optical glasses, which are what photochromic glasses are when clear.</p>
</div>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Can Photochromic Lenses be Used for Driving?</h4>
<p class="para-content">Photochromic lenses <b>can be used as optical glasses when driving during the day, but they may not fully transition into the darker sunglass tint</b>. Photochromic lenses require exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light, which is emitted from the sun, to turn from a clear to a darker tint. According to the Cancer Council, the glass used for the windshield and windows will block one type of UV light called UVB light. The amount of UVA light that is blocked depends on the material used. The ability of the windshield and windows in a car to block UV rays means that photochromic lenses may not work as well as if they have been exposed to direct sunlight. It may be necessary to have separate sunglasses for driving. It is not recommended to wear photochromic lenses for driving at night, as the tint will obscure vision.</p>
</div>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Do Photochromic Lenses Protect Eyes from Blue Light?</h4>
<p class="para-content">Yes, photochromic lenses <b>may protect the eyes from blue light emitted from the sun</b>. <a href="https://www.oscarwylee.com.au/glasses/blue-light">Blue light</a> is a type of visible light, with the highest energy and shortest wavelength according to UC Davis Health. Blue light is emitted from the sun, but artificial blue light can also be found in technology such as phone screens, LED TVs and computers. Sunglasses that have a tint to protect the eyes against UV rays emitted by the sun will typically also protect against blue light emitted by the sun to a degree. Photochromic lenses may similarly protect the eyes from natural blue light when the lens darkens. Photochromic lenses may not protect the eyes from artificial light found in mobile phones, computers and LED TVs.</p>
</div>

<h2 class="subtitle">Who Uses Photochromic Lenses?</h2>
<p class="para-content">The people who use photochromic lenses <b>are typically those who need prescription glasses and do not wish to switch between these glasses and a pair of sunglasses</b>. Prescription glasses refer to glasses that have a power or prescription over the lens. Prescription lenses assist in the correction of refractive errors such as myopia, hyperopia, presbyopia and astigmatism, which can affect vision. If a person has prescription glasses, they may also need sunglasses or prescription sunglasses so they can see clearly when outside in the sun. Photochromic lenses allow these people to have their prescription glasses and sunglasses combined into one.</p>

Photochromic lenses combine prescription glasses and sunglasses into one frame, meaning that people do not need to buy two pairs of glasses or switch between the two when moving indoors and outdoors into the sun</p>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Is it Suggested to Wear Photochromic Lenses All the Time?</h4>
<p class="para-content">No, <b>it is not suggested that a person should wear photochromic lenses all the time, specifically not when driving at night or in low-light conditions</b>. Most windshields and car windows block a significant amount of UV light, which can limit the chemical reaction in photochromic lenses and limit how dark they turn. However, it is still not recommended to wear photochromic lenses, or any sunglasses that have a darker tint, when driving at night or in low-light conditions. Dark lenses at night can obscure vision and make it difficult to see properly.</p>
</div>
<div class="sub-div">
<h4 class="sub-div-title">Can People with Presbyopia Wear Photochromic Lenses?</h4>
<p class="para-content">Yes, people with presbyopia <b>can wear photochromic lenses</b>. <a href="https://www.oscarwylee.com.au/glasses/eye/presbyopia">Presbyopia</a> is an age-related eye condition in which the lens becomes harder and less flexible, leading to blurry near vision. Presbyopia is a refractive error and often requires prescription lenses to help light focus properly on the retina. Photochromic lenses may be a good choice for people with presbyopia who do not wish to switch between their optical glasses and sunglasses.</p>

<p class="para-content">The drawbacks of photochromic lenses include that they are <b>more expensive than normal lenses, they do not work well in a car, and they may darken on cloudy days</b>. The drawbacks of photochromic lenses are listed below.</p>

<h2 class="subtitle">What Differentiates Photochromic Lenses from Other Types of Lenses?</h2>
<p class="para-content">The difference between photochromic lenses and other <a href="https://www.oscarwylee.com.au/glasses/types">types of lenses </a> <b>is their ability to combine the features of both prescription lenses and sunglasses into one lens</b>. <p class="para-content">Photochromic and progressive lenses <b>are similar in that they achieve several functions at once, however, their difference lies in what these functions are</b>.
