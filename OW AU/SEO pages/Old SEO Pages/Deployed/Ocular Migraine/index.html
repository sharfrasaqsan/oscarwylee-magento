Ocular Migraine: Definitions, Causes, Symptoms, Treatments

An ocular migraine is an umbrella term for different types of migraines that involve vision loss and pain, real or perceived, around the eyes or painless spots or blackouts that occur to your vision.

What is an ocular migraine?

An ocular migraine, also known as a retinal migraine, is a term for a painful or inflamed sensation around the eyes and brain. There is no official diagnosis and no condition exactly and precisely called an ‘ocular migraine’. The word refers to many sensations with a similar feeling.

Migraine with aura

The closest diagnosis an optometrist may give for a migraine connected to retinal issues is a ‘migraine with aura’. This migraine is described as a headache that happens before, or during, a time when a patient sees light flashes, sudden blind spots or other feelings around the head.

It is also possible that the visual sensations of flashes of light or a quick moment of blindness occur without any headache or pain associated with them. This type of migraine is referred to as a ‘visual aura’ and they commonly appear as painless flashes of jagged lightning.

What are the symptoms of an ocular migraine?

The following are the common symptoms of what is referred to as an ocular migraine:

  1. Vision issues in one eye
  2. Visual problems with or without a headache
  3. Numbing or tingling on one side of the body
  4. Eye strain
  5. Seeable lights or spots that can affect the eyes’ focal point

If it’s difficult to tell if your vision is only being affected in one eye, try booking an appointment with an optometrist.

What are the symptoms of an ocular migraine?

Triggers of an ocular migraine are completely up to the individual and can be completely variable and tricky to diagnose. The following issues may be the root cause of an ocular migraine, or a migraine with aura:

  1. Bright lights and glare
  2. Many different forms of stress
  3. Excessive alcohol consumption
  4. Not eating regularly
  5. A poor or irregular sleeping schedule

Other causes, including medication or lifestyle changes, are completely possible.

Treating an ocular migraine

Ocular migraines are often treated on a person by person basis. As the cause of the migraine is linked to the individual, the treatment of the migraine can change as well. Stress and lifestyle changes are the most common treatments for the root causes of an ocular migraine. For the immediate symptoms, medication such as aspirin or pain killers can help ease the immediate symptoms. Beta-blockers have shown some effectiveness in reducing the symptoms of ocular migraines, however this is not the medications intended purpose and should not be used without a specialist’s consultation. Additionally, each of these treatments may not affect the cause of the migraine, and must be recommended by a GP or other health professional.

If there is a chance the migraine is caused by stress or eye strain, an optometrist at Oscar Wylee may recommend a blue light filter for your glasses to reduce the bright light and eye strain that may be causing the migraine.

Is an ocular migraine dangerous?

PThere is no type of ocular migraine that will be lethal or dangerous to your health. Ocular migraines are variable depending on the individual in causes and symptoms. This makes it difficult to determine if the symptoms are part of a neurological condition or can be treated as a regular migraine with aura.

Do ocular migraines lead to a stroke?

There is little to no relation between an ocular migraine and a stroke. As a condition where it can be difficult to differentiate whether the migraine is originating from the head or the eyes, it can be very worrying for a patient to have a migraine with aura, due to other, much more severe conditions having similar symptoms.

To understand if a sensation is a stroke instead of an ocular migraine, use the American Stroke Associations F.A.S.T. system. F.A.S.T will help identify the early warning of a stroke before or as they are felt. It stands for:

  • Face: If there are issues such as numbness or tingling on a single side of the face
  • Arm: If one of both arms are numb and can’t be lifted
  • Speech: If speech is jumbled or slurred
  • Time: All these symptoms together must be treated with utmost caution. Do not delay in seeking help

If you are regularly suffering from symptoms of a migraine and unfamiliar sensations begin appearing, consult an expert as soon as possible.

Source: American Stroke Association

When to address your ocular migraine

Many of the symptoms of an ocular migraine can be very alarming to see or look up, but there are important distinctions between an ocular migraine, headaches, or more extreme conditions. By being aware, you can reduce stress and be more aware of your eye health.

Be aware of the signs of a migraine, and not a headache, another type of migraine or deeper issues.

Ocular Migraine Other issues
Visual issues in one or both eyes, with or without a headache Intense headache or painful flashing spots
Vision issues and auras in eyes and head Dizziness and instability
Headaches that rise and eventually fade Fast, sudden and intense headache, lingering pain
Vision changes in only one eye Vision changes in no eyes or both eyes
Symptoms occur gradually Symptoms are fast and unpredictable

It is recommended you see an optometrist regardless of the pain or longevity of your retinal migraine. An eye health professional will be able to see if there are identifiable causes, or if any of your symptoms are an early warning sign for other conditions.
