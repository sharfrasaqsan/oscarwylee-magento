What Are Photochromic Lenses?

Photochromic lenses, also known as transition lenses or adaptive lenses, are a type of prescription glasses that darken in response to contact with ultraviolet rays from the sun. This darkening will even happen on cloudy or rainy days, as the reaction is still active.

William H. Armistead and Stanley Donald Stookey invented the lenses in 1964, but they only saw public use in 1991, with the founding of Transitions Optical.

Why Are They Called Transition Lenses?

The biggest creator and supplier of photochromic lenses is a United States-based company known as Transitions Optical. This company, founded in 1990, is the world’s largest mass producer of photochromic lenses. Because of this, in many parts of the world, they are known by the company’s name for the product.

How Do Photochromic Lenses Work?

Photochromic lenses have a chemical coating in the lens. This chemical is called silver halide, also known as silver chloride. This same material was once used in photographic paper and films. It is a highly UV sensitive substance that shifts shape inside the frame’s lenses in response to the sun’s rays. This causes the lens to darken in response to direct sunlight. The more rays there are, the darker they will become.

Are Photochromic Lens Good For Eyes?

According to Eye Glass Guide, with transition lenses, you have a guarantee of 100% UV blocking sunglasses, saving your eyes from any sun damage exactly the same way a functional pair of good sunnies would.

There is also an added advantage of using photochromic lenses. You not only reduce damage done by UV rays, but you also don’t have to sacrifice your corrective prescription or keep a separate pair of prescription sunglasses for outdoor usage.

Source: Eye Glass Guide

How Do I Know If My Glasses are Photochromic?

There is a straightforward test that can be done if you are unsure if your glasses are photochromic. Simply take the pair you are unsure about and cover them with your hand on a bright day. If they are photochromic, the lens will darken in response to the bright light.

Can You Wear Photochromic Lenses at Night?

As adaptive lenses mainly react to UV rays, they will rarely darken at night, and when they do, it will not be to the extent of daytime rays. This makes them a safe choice of eyewear for driving at night.

However, photochromic lenses will not react to, or aid with glares from artificial sources like headlamps or streetlights, nor will it make any notable difference against bright or disruptive reflections. For functional night driving, it is recommended that you wear a pair of clear prescription or non-prescription glasses (link to be added) with anti-glare coating.

What Are The Disadvantages Of Photochromic Lenses?

Your photochromic lenses may go darker even on a cloudy day, as it is not reacting to light but to UV rays. Depending on your activities when you wear them, this may be an annoyance or a disadvantage. It should also be noted that not all photochromic lenses are polarised, meaning they may not have the desired level of glare blocking at all times.

As carshield windows use the same darkening technique as these lenses, there may sometimes be a clash or redundancy while driving with transition lenses on during the daytime. Photochromic lenses may also need around 30 seconds to adjust to the lighting in order to gain their full effect.

How Long Do Photochromic Lenses Last?

The wear out date of transition lenses depends on a variety of factors, including care and temperature. A pair that is wearing out may begin taking on a yellow tint, and the darkening effect will become less functional.

A good pair of photochromic lenses should typically last for 3 years. With proper care and maintenance, a lifespan of 5 years or more is also possible.
